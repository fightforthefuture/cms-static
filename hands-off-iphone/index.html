Hands Off My iPhone!

New York wants to ban iPhones and any other phone that lets you communicate securely.

Tell lawmakers: Leave my iPhone alone.

Wanting privacy on your phone doesn’t make you a threat. But the New York legislature is considering a bill that would require all secure phones, like iPhones and some Androids, to have surveillance “backdoors” installed. If they get their way, your current phone will be banned and your only option for a new phone in New York would be one that is much less secure.

Secure communication is not a crime.

We use our phones for the most personal things in our lives, and we expect our calls, texts, and emails to stay between us and the people we choose to communicate with. Forcing phone companies to install secret “backdoors” in otherwise secure phones would mean we have no way of knowing if our private communications are actually private at all.

The New York state legislature says they want these backdoors so that law enforcement can break encryption to view the communications of suspected terrorists and criminals, but, as all security-minded individuals understand, once there is a backdoor, anyone can use it. It will be easier for identity thieves or other people who seek to do you harm to invade your privacy and steal your information.

Even worse, the government’s massive wiretapping programs, conducted by the NSA and the FBI, are so broad that these backdoors could be used to conduct bulk surveillance operations on massive swathes of the populations. Imagine if every iPhone in Zuccotti Park had a backdoor while the Occupy movement was trying to organize and evade violent police attacks.
