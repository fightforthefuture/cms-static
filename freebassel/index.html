Only the Internet can save Bassel Khartabi

Bassel Khartabil has been detained arbitrarily since 2012 in what seems like an attempt by the Syrian government to stifle free speech. Bassel's work centered around setting up a shared networking space in Damascus and Creative Commons Syria. Bassel also contributed code to projects we love like Mozilla and Wikipedia so that he could help advance freedom of expression in Syria — a country with a notorious record of online censorship. He has received a number of awards including the 2013 Index on Censorship Digital Freedom Award and was named one of Foreign Policy magazine's Top 100 Global Thinkers of 2012, “for insisting, against all odds, on a peaceful Syrian revolution.” He was offered a position at MIT right before he went missing.

The United Nations Working Group on Arbitrary Detention has declared Bassel’s detention as a violation of the International Convenant on Civil and Political rights.

The Internet community is uniting to help fight for Bassel’s life and demand that he be freed, and it is working. Bassel was first taken to a secret military prison that Human Rights Watch calls a “torture archipelago” in 2012. But, the Electronic Frontier Foundation, Mozilla, Creative Commons, and others, stepped up and he was moved to a civilian prison where we could monitor his well being.

Now, his whereabouts are unknown and rumors from sources close to the regime say he may be sentenced to death.

"The Internet Governance Forum in João Pessoa, Brazil, has released a statement demanding that the Syrian government alert Bassel's family to his whereabouts and exercise clemency in his case. We urge the internet community to exercise whatever pressure we can on the Syrian government to make Bassel's whereabouts known and release him from detention." [3]

Syria should move to pardon Bassel as a show of good will, and as Joi Ito said, "a contribution to the preservation of Syrian culture." [4]
